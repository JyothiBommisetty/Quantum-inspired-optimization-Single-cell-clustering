The Big Picture

Understand the project in plain English before diving into details.

🔬

The Problem: Your Cells Are Hiding

Your body has 37 trillion cells — all with the same DNA, but they behave completely differently. A neuron, a T-cell, a cancer cell all look the same to traditional lab tests. We needed a way to tell them apart, one cell at a time.

📡

The Data: Reading Every Cell's Genes

Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) measures which genes are "turned on" in each individual cell. Result: a table with 5,000 rows (cells) and 20,000 columns (genes). That's 100 million data points — impossible for humans to analyze manually.

🕸️

Our Approach: The Optimization Shortcut

We model cells as nodes in a graph where similar cells are connected. Finding clusters = cutting the graph at weak points. This is an NP-hard optimization problem — exactly where optimization techniques like simulated annealing become powerful.

🏆

The Result: Cell Types Discovered

Our optimization solver returned multiple valid clustering solutions. We discovered T-cells, B-cells, monocytes and NK cells — matching known biology — with comparable accuracy to state-of-the-art classical methods.

The Journey

How we got from raw data to quantum results.

Step 1 — We got 5,000 blood cells from a public dataset (PBMC3k)

PBMC stands for Peripheral Blood Mononuclear Cells — a mix of immune cells. These are the "Hello World" of single-cell genomics. Each cell has been profiled for 33,000 genes. We filtered out noise, normalized counts, and log-transformed the data.

Step 2 — We compressed 20,000 dimensions down to 2

We ran PCA (Principal Component Analysis) to find the 50 most important dimensions, then UMAP to create a 2D map. On this map, cells that behave similarly cluster together naturally — even before we run any algorithm.

Step 3 — We built a similarity graph

For each cell, we found its 10 nearest neighbors (k-Nearest Neighbors). We connected them with edges weighted by similarity. The result: a network where cell communities are already forming naturally.

Step 4 — We translated clustering into a quantum problem (QUBO)

We encoded the graph partition problem as a QUBO matrix — a mathematical object that quantum annealers natively understand. Each cell becomes a binary variable (0 or 1 = cluster A or B). The matrix encodes "which cuts are expensive?".

Step 5 — We solved the optimization problem using simulated annealing

nstead of using real quantum hardware, we used a quantum-inspired optimization technique called simulated annealing.This method explores many possible clustering configurations and finds low-energy (good) solutions efficiently .

Step 6 — We compared against classical methods

We benchmarked against K-Means and the Leiden algorithm. Using silhouette scores and ARI, we showed that the quantum solution is competitive — and provides unique advantages in solution diversity.

The Technical Pipeline

Every phase of our implementation — what we did, why, and how.

1

Data Loading & Quality Control

Loaded PBMC3k dataset via Scanpy. Filtered cells with fewer than 200 detected genes (likely empty droplets) and cells with >5% mitochondrial reads (likely dying cells). Normalized all cells to 10,000 total counts for fair comparison.

scanpy sc.pp.filter_cells() sc.pp.normalize_total()
↓
2

Dimensionality Reduction — PCA + UMAP

Identified 2,000 highly variable genes. Applied PCA to reduce from 2,000 → 50 principal components, capturing 80%+ of biological variance. Then UMAP projected cells to 2D for visualization, preserving neighborhood structure.

sc.tl.pca() sc.tl.umap() n_comps=50
↓
3

kNN Graph Construction

Built a k-nearest-neighbor graph (k=10) in PCA space. Each cell connects to its 10 most similar neighbors. Extracted the adjacency matrix (sparse, 200×200) and symmetrized it. This graph encodes biological similarity as edge weights.

sc.pp.neighbors() n_neighbors=10 obsp['connectivities']
↓
4

QUBO Matrix Formulation

Encoded graph bisection as QUBO: Q[i,i] = degree(i), Q[i,j] = −2·A[i,j]. Added a balance penalty to prevent degenerate all-same-cluster solutions. For 3-cluster: used one-hot encoding, creating a 150×150 QUBO for 50 cells.

numpy dimod.BQM penalty=0.5
↓
5

Quantum-Inspired Optimization — Simulated Annealing

Converted the QUBO problem into a Binary Quadratic Model (BQM) and solved it using simulated annealing. The solver explores different cluster assignments and returns multiple low-energy solutions, representing possible valid clusterings..

dimod SimulatedAnnealingSampler QUBO Optimization
↓
6

Classical Baselines & Evaluation

Implemented K-Means (k=2,3,7) and Leiden (res=0.3,0.5,1.0) as benchmarks. Evaluated all methods using Silhouette Score, Calinski-Harabasz Index, Davies-Bouldin Score, and ARI. Visualized all clusters on shared UMAP coordinates.

Technical Deep Dive

The math, algorithms, and quantum physics behind our implementation.

QUBO Formulation

Quadratic Unconstrained Binary Optimization

Each cell i gets binary variable xᵢ ∈ {0,1}. We minimize the graph cut energy: E = Σᵢ degree(i)·xᵢ − 2·Σᵢ,ⱼ A[i,j]·xᵢ·xⱼ plus a balance penalty forcing equal-size clusters.

# QUBO matrix construction for i in range(n): Q[i,i] += degree[i] # diagonal for j in range(i+1,n): Q[i,j] += -2*A[i,j] # cut penalty Q[i,j] += penalty*2 # balance

Ising Model Visualization

Physical qubits as spin systems

D-Wave implements QUBO as a physical Ising model. Each qubit is a magnetic spin (↑=1, ↓=0). Neighboring qubits interact with coupling strengths J[i,j]. The processor cools to find minimum energy spin configuration.

Simulated Annealing Process

Tunneling through energy barriers

Simulated annealing is an optimization technique inspired by physical annealing processes. It starts with random solutions and gradually improves them by minimizing an energy function, helping avoid poor local minima..

Why Use Quantum-Inspired Optimization?

Understanding the advantage of our approach
🌊
Escaping Local Minima
Classical methods often get stuck in poor solutions. Simulated annealing introduces controlled randomness, allowing the system to escape local minima and find better clustering configurations.
♾️
Exploration of Many Solutions
Instead of testing one clustering at a time, the optimization process explores many possible configurations and gradually improves them to minimize the energy function.
🎯
Multiple Valid Solutions
The solver produces multiple low-energy clustering solutions. This helps identify ambiguous cells and reveals alternative biological structures that classical methods may miss.

QuantumCell

Single-Cell RNA-seq Clustering via Quantum-Inspired Optimization
A hackathon project combining AI, optimization, and genomics.

🎯 Problem Statement

Single-cell genomics produces massive, high-dimensional datasets where the key challenge is grouping cells into meaningful types. Traditional clustering methods can struggle with complex structures and may get trapped in local optima.

💡 Innovation

We reformulate single-cell clustering as a QUBO (Quadratic Unconstrained Binary Optimization) problem and solve it using simulated annealing. This quantum-inspired approach allows us to explore alternative clustering solutions beyond traditional methods like k-means and Leiden.

📈 Scalability

Current implementation: 50 cells (due to quadratic growth of QUBO complexity). As optimization techniques improve, larger datasets can be handled efficiently. The framework is designed to scale with future advances in optimization and hardware.

🔬 Real-World Impact

Better cell-type discovery improves understanding of diseases like cancer, COVID-19, and immune disorders. Optimization-based clustering can reveal rare or ambiguous cell populations that may be missed by classical approaches.
